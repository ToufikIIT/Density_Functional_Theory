1.3.4 Exchange and Correlation Energy

One of the central strengths of Density Functional Theory (DFT) lies in how it accounts for the complex quantum mechanical effects of many-electron systems using the electron density \(n(\vec{r})\) as its primary variable. A key piece of this is the exchange-correlation energy functional, denoted \(E_{xc}[n]\). This term compactly represents the difference between the true many-body quantum energy and what is captured by a simple non-interacting reference system.

The exchange-correlation energy functional is generally split into two parts:

\[ E_{xc}[n] = E_x[n] + E_c[n] \tag{1.64} \]

Here:

  • Exchange energy \(E_x[n]\) arises from the antisymmetry of the wavefunction and is present even in the absence of electron correlation.
  • Correlation energy \(E_c[n]\) captures the extra interactions due to the dynamic behavior of electrons avoiding each other due to Coulomb repulsion.

üîÅ Exchange Energy

The exchange energy can be formally written as:

\[ E_x[n] = \langle \Phi^{\text{min}}_s | \hat{V}_{ee} | \Phi^{\text{min}}_s \rangle - U[n] \tag{1.65} \]

Where:

  • \( \Phi^{\text{min}}_s \): The Kohn‚ÄìSham determinant minimizing the kinetic energy among non-interacting wavefunctions yielding density \(n\).
  • \( \hat{V}_{ee} \): Electron‚Äìelectron interaction operator.
  • \( U[n] \): The classical Hartree energy (i.e., electron‚Äìelectron repulsion calculated via Coulomb's law).

Physically, this equation subtracts the classical electrostatic energy to avoid counting electron self-repulsion.

Then, the total energy in this approximation becomes:

\[ \langle \Phi^{\text{min}}_s | \hat{T} + \hat{V}_{ext} + \hat{V}_{ee} | \Phi^{\text{min}}_s \rangle = T_s[n] + U[n] + E_x[n] \tag{1.66} \]

Notably, for one-electron systems:

\[ E_x[n] = -U[n] \quad (N = 1) \tag{1.67} \]

ensuring exact cancellation of the unphysical self-interaction from \(U[n]\).

üîó Correlation Energy

The correlation energy accounts for the additional complexity in real many-electron systems:

\[ E_c[n] = F[n] - (T_s[n] + U[n] + E_x[n]) \tag{1.68} \]

It can also be seen as:

\[ E_c[n] = \langle \Psi | \hat{T} + \hat{V}_{ee} | \Psi \rangle - \langle \Phi^{\text{min}}_s | \hat{T} + \hat{V}_{ee} | \Phi^{\text{min}}_s \rangle \tag{1.68'} \]

Here \(\Psi\) is the exact interacting ground state. The difference between interacting and non-interacting expectations defines \(E_c[n]\).

\[ E_c[n] \leq 0 \tag{1.69} \]
\[ E_c[n] = 0 \quad (N = 1) \tag{1.70} \]

Thus, the correlation energy is always non-positive and vanishes for a single electron ‚Äî a useful boundary condition for testing functionals.

‚öñÔ∏è Self-Interaction Cancellation

The design of accurate \(E_{xc}[n]\) functionals ensures cancellation of unphysical self-interaction energies. This includes both the classical self-Coulomb term \(U[n]\) and related spurious exchange terms.

\[ \frac{\delta E_x[n]}{\delta n(\vec{r})} = -u([n]; \vec{r}) \tag{1.71} \]

And for the one-electron limit:

\[ \frac{\delta E_c[n]}{\delta n(\vec{r})} = 0 \quad (N = 1) \tag{1.72} \]
\[ \lim_{r \to \infty} \frac{\delta E_{xc}[n]}{\delta n(r)} = -\frac{1}{r} \tag{1.73} \]

üìå Summary

In summary, the exchange-correlation energy functional:

  • Bridges the gap between non-interacting reference systems and real quantum systems.
  • Encodes the effects of antisymmetry and dynamic electron correlation.
  • Is central to the predictive power and accuracy of DFT.
