1.1.2 Summary of Kohn‚ÄìSham Spin-Density Functional Theory

This section presents a summary of the Kohn‚ÄìSham spin-density functional method, the most widely used approach in condensed-matter physics and quantum chemistry for electronic structure calculations. It simplifies the problem of many-body interactions by introducing non-interacting reference systems.

We are concerned with finding the ground-state total energy \( E \) and spin densities \( n_\uparrow(\vec{r}), n_\downarrow(\vec{r}) \) of a system of \( N \) interacting electrons in an external potential \( v(\vec{r}) \).

üßÆ Kohn‚ÄìSham Equations

The Kohn‚ÄìSham approach rewrites the many-body problem in terms of a set of single-particle equations. These auxiliary equations involve an effective potential and are easier to solve.

\[ \left( -\frac{1}{2} \nabla^2 + v(\vec{r}) + u[n](\vec{r}) + v_{xc}^{\sigma}([n_\uparrow,n_\downarrow];\vec{r}) \right) \varphi_{\alpha\sigma}(\vec{r}) = \varepsilon_{\alpha\sigma} \varphi_{\alpha\sigma}(\vec{r}) \]

(1.1) ‚Äî Kohn‚ÄìSham equation with effective potential for each spin orbital

\[ n_\sigma(\vec{r}) = \sum_\alpha \theta(\mu - \varepsilon_{\alpha\sigma}) |\varphi_{\alpha\sigma}(\vec{r})|^2 \]

(1.2) ‚Äî Defines spin density as a sum over occupied orbitals

\[ u[n](\vec{r}) = \int d^3r' \frac{n(\vec{r}')}{|\vec{r} - \vec{r}'|} \]

(1.3) ‚Äî Classical Hartree potential: electron‚Äìelectron repulsion

\[ n(\vec{r}) = n_\uparrow(\vec{r}) + n_\downarrow(\vec{r}) \]

(1.4) ‚Äî Total electronic density from spin components

üîã Total Energy Functional

The total energy of the interacting system is expressed as a sum of terms depending on spin densities:

\[ E = T_s[n_\uparrow,n_\downarrow] + \int d^3r\, n(\vec{r})v(\vec{r}) + U[n] + E_{xc}[n_\uparrow,n_\downarrow] \]

(1.6) ‚Äî Total energy functional in Kohn‚ÄìSham formalism

Where each term is:

  • \( T_s \): kinetic energy of non-interacting reference system
  • \( \int n v \): interaction with external potential
  • \( U[n] \): Hartree repulsion
  • \( E_{xc} \): exchange‚Äìcorrelation energy
\[ T_s[n_\uparrow,n_\downarrow] = \sum_\alpha \theta(\mu - \varepsilon_{\alpha\sigma})\langle \varphi_{\alpha\sigma}|(-\frac{1}{2}\nabla^2)|\varphi_{\alpha\sigma} \rangle \]

(1.7) ‚Äî Non-interacting kinetic energy

\[ \langle \varphi |\hat{O}| \varphi \rangle = \int d^3r\, \varphi^*(\vec{r})\hat{O}\varphi(\vec{r}) \]

(1.8) ‚Äî General expectation value formula

\[ U[n] = \frac{1}{2}\int d^3r \int d^3r' \frac{n(\vec{r})n(\vec{r}')}{|\vec{r} - \vec{r}'|} \]

(1.9) ‚Äî Electrostatic (Hartree) interaction energy

\[ v_{xc}^\sigma([n_\uparrow,n_\downarrow]; \vec{r}) = \frac{\delta E_{xc}[n_\uparrow,n_\downarrow]}{\delta n_\sigma(\vec{r})} \]

(1.10) ‚Äî Exchange‚Äìcorrelation potential as functional derivative

üî¨ Exchange‚ÄìCorrelation Approximations

Since the exact form of \( E_{xc} \) is unknown, approximations are crucial for practical applications:

Local Spin Density Approximation (LSD)

\[ E_{xc}^{LSD}[n_\uparrow,n_\downarrow] = \int d^3r\, n(\vec{r})\varepsilon_{xc}(n_\uparrow(\vec{r}), n_\downarrow(\vec{r})) \]

(1.11) ‚Äî Uses local value of the densities

Generalized Gradient Approximation (GGA)

\[ E_{xc}^{GGA}[n_\uparrow,n_\downarrow] = \int d^3r\, f(n_\uparrow, n_\downarrow, \nabla n_\uparrow, \nabla n_\downarrow) \]

(1.12) ‚Äî Includes density gradients for improved accuracy

GGA improves upon LSD by including variations in density, leading to better predictions for molecular properties like atomization energy, bond lengths, and dissociation energies.

üìå Key Insights

  • The exact \( E_{xc} \) depends on both densities and their gradients; practical use requires approximations.
  • While \( E_{xc} \) is small relative to total energy, its accurate approximation is crucial for meaningful results.
  • LSD and GGA form the basis of modern DFT codes.
  • The method simplifies the complex many-electron problem to a self-consistent iteration of single-particle equations.
