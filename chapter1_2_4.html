1.2.4 Hellmann–Feynman Theorem

The Hellmann–Feynman theorem relates the derivative of the energy of a quantum system with respect to a parameter to the expectation value of the derivative of the Hamiltonian. This is particularly useful when computing forces in quantum systems.

If the Hamiltonian \( \hat{H}_\lambda \) depends on a parameter \( \lambda \), then for a normalized eigenfunction \( \Psi_\lambda \), the energy \( E_\lambda \) is given by:

\[ E_\lambda = \langle \Psi_\lambda | \hat{H}_\lambda | \Psi_\lambda \rangle \quad \text{(1.33)} \]

Then the derivative of energy with respect to \( \lambda \) is:

\[ \frac{dE_\lambda}{d\lambda} = \left.\frac{d}{d\lambda} \langle \Psi_\lambda | \hat{H}_\lambda | \Psi_\lambda \rangle \right|_{\lambda} + \left\langle \Psi_\lambda \left| \frac{\partial \hat{H}_\lambda}{\partial \lambda} \right| \Psi_\lambda \right\rangle \quad \text{(1.34)} \]

By the variational principle, the first term vanishes, and we get the Hellmann–Feynman theorem:

\[ \frac{dE_\lambda}{d\lambda} = \left\langle \Psi_\lambda \left| \frac{\partial \hat{H}_\lambda}{\partial \lambda} \right| \Psi_\lambda \right\rangle \quad \text{(1.35)} \]

Application to Electrostatic Forces

This result is central to computing forces on nuclei in quantum chemistry. Let \( \vec{r}_i \) denote the position of the \( i \)-th electron and \( \vec{R}_I \) that of the \( I \)-th nucleus (with atomic number \( Z_I \)). The full Hamiltonian becomes:

\[ \hat{H} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \nabla_i^2 + \sum_{i=1}^N v(\vec{r}_i) - \sum_{i,I} \frac{Z_I}{|\vec{r}_i - \vec{R}_I|} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i \ne j} \frac{1}{|\vec{r}_i - \vec{r}_j|} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{I \ne J} \frac{Z_I Z_J}{|\vec{R}_I - \vec{R}_J|} \quad \text{(1.36)} \]

To calculate the force on nucleus \( I \), we compute the derivative of total energy with respect to \( \vec{R}_I \):

\[ -\frac{\partial E}{\partial \vec{R}_I} = \left\langle \Psi \left| \frac{\partial \hat{H}}{\partial \vec{R}_I} \right| \Psi \right\rangle \]

This leads to:

\[ = \int d^3r \, n(\vec{r}) \frac{Z_I (\vec{r} - \vec{R}_I)}{|\vec{r} - \vec{R}_I|^3} + \sum_{J \ne I} \frac{Z_I Z_J (\vec{R}_I - \vec{R}_J)}{|\vec{R}_I - \vec{R}_J|^3} \quad \text{(1.37)} \]

This gives the electrostatic force on nucleus \( I \) from electrons and other nuclei. Thus, the Hellmann–Feynman theorem enables direct computation of forces from quantum-mechanical expectation values.
