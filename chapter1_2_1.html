1.2.1 Wavefunctions and Their Interpretation

This section introduces fundamental concepts of quantum mechanics relevant to DFT. We start with the description of one-particle states and gradually extend to many-particle systems, ultimately connecting wavefunctions to the electron density.

üîπ One-Particle Quantum States

Each electron has a spin quantum number \( s = 1/2 \), and the spin projection along the z-axis is \( \sigma = \pm 1/2 \), often denoted \( \uparrow \) or \( \downarrow \).

The Hamiltonian (energy operator) for an electron in an external potential \( v(\vec{r}) \) is given by:

\[ \hat{h} = -\frac{1}{2} \nabla^2 + v(\vec{r}) \tag{1.13} \]

The Schr√∂dinger equation defines the energy eigenstates \( \psi_\alpha(\vec{r}, \sigma) \) and eigenvalues \( \varepsilon_\alpha \):

\[ \hat{h}\psi_\alpha(\vec{r}, \sigma) = \varepsilon_\alpha \psi_\alpha(\vec{r}, \sigma) \tag{1.14} \]

üîπ Probability and Normalization

The quantity \( |\psi_\alpha(\vec{r},\sigma)|^2 \) gives the probability of finding an electron with spin \( \sigma \) at position \( \vec{r} \). The normalization condition is:

\[ \sum_\sigma \int d^3r \; |\psi_\alpha(\vec{r},\sigma)|^2 = 1 \tag{1.15} \]

üîπ Many-Electron Systems

We generalize to \( N \) interacting electrons. The total Hamiltonian becomes:

\[ \hat{H} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \nabla_i^2 + \sum_{i=1}^N v(\vec{r}_i) + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i \ne j} \frac{1}{|\vec{r}_i - \vec{r}_j|} \tag{1.16} \]

The last term represents the electron-electron repulsion.

The eigenstates \( \Psi_k(\vec{r}_1\sigma_1, \ldots, \vec{r}_N\sigma_N) \) satisfy:

\[ \hat{H} \Psi_k = E_k \Psi_k \tag{1.17} \]

üîπ Fermionic Nature and Antisymmetry

Electrons are fermions. Thus, the wavefunction must be antisymmetric under exchange of any two particles:

\[ \Psi(\ldots, \vec{r}_i \sigma_i, \ldots, \vec{r}_j \sigma_j, \ldots) = -\Psi(\ldots, \vec{r}_j \sigma_j, \ldots, \vec{r}_i \sigma_i, \ldots) \tag{1.18} \]

This property ensures the Pauli exclusion principle and results in an antisymmetric structure of the total wavefunction.

üîπ Normalization in Many-Body Systems

The many-body wavefunction \( \Psi \) must also be normalized. Considering all spin labels and configurations:

\[ \frac{1}{N!} \sum_{\sigma_1 \ldots \sigma_N} \int d^3r_1 \cdots d^3r_N \; N! |\Psi(\vec{r}_1 \sigma_1, \ldots, \vec{r}_N \sigma_N)|^2 = 1 \tag{1.19} \]

üîπ Spin Density

The spin-resolved electron density \( n_\sigma(\vec{r}) \) is defined as the probability of finding an electron with spin \( \sigma \) at position \( \vec{r} \):

\[ n_\sigma(\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{(N-1)!} \sum_{\sigma_2 \ldots \sigma_N} \int d^3r_2 \cdots d^3r_N \; N |\Psi(\vec{r}\sigma, \vec{r}_2\sigma_2, \ldots, \vec{r}_N\sigma_N)|^2 \tag{1.20} \]

This marginalizes over all electrons except one, giving us the local density at \( \vec{r} \).

üîπ Sum Rule

Integrating \( n_\sigma(\vec{r}) \) over all space and summing over spins gives the total number of electrons:

\[ \sum_\sigma \int d^3r \; n_\sigma(\vec{r}) = N \tag{1.21} \]

üîπ Expectation Value of the External Potential

We can write the expectation value of the external potential energy as:

\[ \langle \hat{V}_{\text{ext}} \rangle = \langle \Psi | \sum_{i=1}^N v(\vec{r}_i) | \Psi \rangle = \int d^3r \; n(\vec{r}) v(\vec{r}) \tag{1.22} \]

Where the total electron density \( n(\vec{r}) = n_\uparrow(\vec{r}) + n_\downarrow(\vec{r}) \) (see Eq. (1.4)).
