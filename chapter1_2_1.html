1.2.1 Wavefunctions and Their Interpretation

In quantum mechanics, the physical state of an isolated system of electrons is described by a many-body wavefunction \( \Psi(\vec{r}_1, \ldots, \vec{r}_N) \). This complex-valued function is square-integrable and encodes all physical observables of the system.

Normalization

The wavefunction must satisfy the normalization condition:

\[ \int d^3r_1 \cdots d^3r_N \; |\Psi(\vec{r}_1, \ldots, \vec{r}_N)|^2 = 1 \]

This ensures that the total probability of finding the electrons somewhere in space is unity.

Antisymmetry and Fermions

Because electrons are indistinguishable fermions, the wavefunction must be antisymmetric under the exchange of any two electron coordinates:

\[ \Psi(\ldots, \vec{r}_i, \ldots, \vec{r}_j, \ldots) = -\Psi(\ldots, \vec{r}_j, \ldots, \vec{r}_i, \ldots) \]

This antisymmetry leads to the Pauli exclusion principle, which prevents any two electrons from occupying the same quantum state.

Observables from the Wavefunction

All measurable physical quantities (observables) of the system, such as energy or electron density, can be computed from \( \Psi \). In particular, the one-particle density is a key quantity in DFT, defined as:

\[ n(\vec{r}) = N \int d^3r_2 \ldots d^3r_N \; |\Psi(\vec{r}, \vec{r}_2, \ldots, \vec{r}_N)|^2 \]

This gives the probability density of finding one electron at position \( \vec{r} \), independent of the positions of the other electrons.

Interpretation of \( |\Psi|^2 \)

The quantity \( |\Psi(\vec{r}_1, \ldots, \vec{r}_N)|^2 \) represents the probability density of finding the first electron near \( \vec{r}_1 \), the second near \( \vec{r}_2 \), and so on. This probabilistic interpretation is foundational to quantum mechanics.

Why Use Densities?

Although \( \Psi \) contains complete information, it depends on \( 3N \) variables, making it computationally intractable for large \( N \). In contrast, the one-particle density \( n(\vec{r}) \) depends only on 3 variables and is the basic quantity in DFT. The central idea of DFT is that all ground-state properties of a system are functionals of \( n(\vec{r}) \), not \( \Psi \).
