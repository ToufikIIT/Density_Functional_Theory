1.1.1 Quantum Mechanical Many-Electron Problem

The material world we experience is fundamentally composed of electrons and nuclei. Most physical, chemical, and condensed matter properties emerge from the behavior of electrons. Even though a few (or many) nuclei are involved, it's the electrons that primarily govern observable behavior.

‚öõÔ∏è Electrostatic Interactions and Quantum Description

  • At the atomic scale, electrons and nuclei interact through Coulomb forces.
  • For a nucleus with charge \( Z \) at position \( \vec{R} \), and an electron at \( \vec{r} \), the electrostatic potential energy is: \[ V(\vec{r}) = -\frac{Z}{|\vec{r} - \vec{R}|} \]
  • Two nuclei at positions \( \vec{R} \) and \( \vec{R}' \) repel each other via \( Z^2 / |\vec{R} - \vec{R}'| \).

Electrons cannot be described by classical mechanics due to their microscopic nature. Instead, quantum mechanics is required. This includes treatment of them as indistinguishable fermions obeying the Pauli exclusion principle.

In lighter elements, valence electrons often move at speeds significantly less than light, allowing us to ignore relativistic corrections.

üî¨ The Goal of Theory

Theory seeks to derive material properties (molecular structure, chemical reactivity, solid-state behavior) from basic physical principles. In particular, ground-state properties are of utmost interest.

This gives rise to Density Functional Theory (DFT) ‚Äî a formalism that reformulates quantum mechanics based on the electron density \( \rho(\vec{r}) \), rather than the many-body wave function \( \Psi(\vec{r}_1, ..., \vec{r}_N) \).

üìò What This Text Covers

This primer focuses on introducing DFT for non-relativistic Coulomb systems. The reader is expected to have some background in quantum mechanics, particularly in working with one-particle wavefunctions \( \psi(\vec{r}) \).

The many-body wavefunction \( \Psi(\vec{r}_1, \dots, \vec{r}_N) \) is introduced and replaced by the electron density \( n(\vec{r}) \). All relevant energy terms become functionals of this density.

The two most widely used approximations to the energy functional are:

  • Local Spin Density Approximation (LSDA)
  • Generalized Gradient Approximation (GGA)
