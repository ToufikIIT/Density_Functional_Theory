1.2.3 Wavefunction Variational Principle

The variational principle is a cornerstone of quantum mechanics. It provides a method to approximate the ground state energy and wavefunction by minimizing the expectation value of the Hamiltonian under the normalization constraint:

\[ \delta \left\{ \frac{\langle \Psi | \hat{H} | \Psi \rangle}{\langle \Psi | \Psi \rangle} \right\} = 0 \]

(1.30) ‚Äî Variational principle with normalization

This is used to find the minimum energy configuration of a quantum system. The Rayleigh‚ÄìRitz method searches for this minimum over a restricted space of wavefunctions. One widely used approximation in quantum chemistry is the Hartree‚ÄìFock method, which finds the Slater determinant that minimizes the expectation value \( \langle \Phi | \hat{H} | \Phi \rangle / \langle \Phi | \Phi \rangle \).

More refined methods include:

  • Configuration interaction (CI): Combines multiple Slater determinants.
  • Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC): Uses stochastic sampling with trial wavefunctions such as a Slater determinant times a correlation factor (e.g., Jastrow).

These methods are computationally demanding, especially for large systems, which is where Density Functional Theory (DFT) becomes valuable.

üîì Lagrange Multiplier Formulation

To handle the normalization constraint, we introduce a Lagrange multiplier \( E(\Psi) \) and reformulate the variational principle as an unconstrained optimization:

\[ \delta \left\{ \langle \Psi | \hat{H} | \Psi \rangle - E(\Psi) \langle \Psi | \Psi \rangle \right\} = 0 \]

(1.31) ‚Äî Unconstrained formulation using Lagrange multipliers

This leads to the Schr√∂dinger equation as a condition for extremization:

\[ \langle \delta \Psi | (\hat{H} - E) | \Psi \rangle = 0 \]

(1.32) ‚Äî Equivalent to time-independent Schr√∂dinger equation

üìå Remarks

  • This formalism recovers the Schr√∂dinger equation \( \hat{H}\Psi = E\Psi \).
  • Every eigenstate of \( \hat{H} \) is an extremum of the energy functional.
  • This principle also supports the derivation of the Hellmann‚ÄìFeynman theorem.
